EVF-1 Saving File for Reader

Description of EVF-1

  • When reading the stories created by users, the program should save where in the story the reader is and what choices have been made so far.
  • We will test whether the chapter automatically saves into our database. This will also test whether the saves will automatically overwrite and save any previous progress.

User Story

As a creator using Text-to-Game, I should be able to confirm that my program saves where the reader last was and what choices have been made so far when they are playing the game. The users playing my game should not have to start from the very beginning each time they want to play. When they play a game made by Text-to-Game, there should be a correct sense of time and progression.

Lucid Textual Explanation

When playing a chapter as a player, the autosave feature will periodically save the user’s progress. This feature helps to minimise data loss and attempts to recover game progress in case of a system failure or crash. The autosave feature creates a save state periodically/upon every change within the game and stores it into a database. When the user continues playing the game, the game retrieves the stored data and loads the most recent save state. It is important that a system reliably functions as intended and that information is stored and retrieved correctly.

EVF-2 Inserting Custom Images

User Stories

As an author, I want to be able to insert images whilst creating my text-to-game. Being able to insert images allows for extra immersion from the player as they can visually see what is happening within the game. Inserting custom images would be a vital function due to the customizable and limitless options available to myself, the author.

Lucid Textual Explanation

Inserting custom images for the user to see allows the player to visually capture what the game is telling them. Although text-to-game is a simple text-based game where the player progresses based on choosing their path via text options, the functionality to visually see what their paths come to fruition is a feature that we would like to implement into text-to-game. The author would be able to insert their own images into text-to-game whilst they are creating their game and the users would therefore be able to view these images once they arrive at that specific level. The author can dictate what images are shown and when they’d like to show the image. For example, if the player were to choose between buying a sword or a bow, there should be an image for both depending on what the player would like to purchase.

Validation Testing

There are many different validation methods that must be followed to ensure that the program is working properly in text-to-game. For example, if custom images were to be inserted by the author, the image would have to have the correct resolution and size for the program to work effectively. There would have to be a system of standardization and consistency within most of the images to ensure that each stage and level maintains visual quality. As a result, the text-to-game program would preferably have an alert to tell the author what the minimum size or resolution must be for text-to-game to function effectively. This validation would occur after the author has inserted their custom image and if it is not aligned with the system’s standards, an alert would appear notifying the user. Furthermore, the image must be in the correct format which is typically the jpeg format. The jpeg format is the most used format with regards to images and the author would be notified if their image file was not compatible with the text-to-game program.

Validation Criteria

  • Are the image resolutions consistent throughout the program?
  • Does the image need to be scaled?
  • Does pressing the ‘Insert Image’ button work?
  • Is the image compatible?
  • Does the image resolution fit the program?
  • Can images be deleted?
  • Is there a limit to how many images can be inserted?

Story

This example utilises all of the core functionalities of our program in that it allows users to choose their pathways and endings. This demonstration was designed for a construction company to teach new apprentices the importance of safety while working the job. This example shows how the program is able to give users options to choose from if it is required from the story, the outcomes of those actions and the flow between chapters. This also showcases how 2 chapters can extend into multiple different pathways with minimal limitations.

Chapter 1

A new apprentice named Hairy Jack has joined the workforce and will be commencing his first shift next week. He has discovered that his carry bag can only hold enough room for his essential equipment and can’t decide what he doesn’t need to bring to work. Can you help Hairy Jack decide what he doesn’t need to pack in his bag for him?

1: High visibility clothes

2: Helmet

3: Coffee

If 1 or 2 was selected:

Hairy Jack has now packed his bags and is ready to go to work. He arrives to his first shift where he is greeted to punishment for forgetting his essential work equipment. Hairy Jack has now been sent home as a result of his actions. Hairy Jack no longer wants your help.

Story finished.

If 3 was selected:

Hairy Jack has now packed his bags and is ready to go to work. He arrives at his first shift where he is congratulated by his boss for packing all the required equipment and is rewarded with a coffee.

Next Chapter.

Chapter 2

While Hairy Jack consumes his coffee, he soon trips over his shoelaces and spills his coffee all over the floor at the construction site. Jack doesn’t want to get into trouble, so he decides to clean it up himself without telling his boss. You notice Hairy Jack tripping over and spilling his coffee but can’t decide how to help. You are required to act in an effective manner that will benefit the team.

1: Help clean up the coffee

2: Tell your safety manager

3: Tell your boss

If 2 or 3 was selected

Notifying anyone in charge of any safety hazards is required of you, thus telling either your safety manager or boss about the accident is a suitable approach. You are both rewarded with coffee and no one was injured.

Story Ends

You have chosen to not notify your team about the accident, your boss notices the spill and approaches you both with a look of confusion.
